# From Garage R&D to NYSE Exit

*The Resonado Labs journey: patents, partnerships, and manufacturing*

---

In 2016, I co-founded Resonado Labs — a deep-tech audio company. In 2022, we exited to LCI Industries (NYSE: LCII). 

The journey from garage-stage R&D to strategic acquisition taught me more about building companies than any business school could. Here's what I learned about translating research into revenue.

## The Origin: First-Principles Physics

Resonado started with a question: why do speakers look the same as they did 50 years ago?

Traditional speakers use a voice coil attached to a cone. The coil moves back and forth in a magnetic field, pushing air to create sound. This design has fundamental limitations:
- Requires depth for cone excursion
- Inefficient energy transfer
- Difficult to create directional audio

We developed a planar transducer architecture that moved air differently. Instead of a cone, we used a flat surface that flexed in controlled patterns. The physics allowed for:
- Dramatically thinner form factors
- Better directional control
- Improved efficiency at certain frequencies

This became the basis for 5 U.S. utility patents.

## The First Hard Lesson: Research ≠ Product

Having novel technology meant nothing without a path to market.

Our first year, we made a classic deep-tech mistake: we kept optimizing the technology without validating market demand. We could make our transducer 10% more efficient, or 5% thinner, or extend the frequency range slightly. None of these improvements mattered if no one wanted to buy it.

**The shift:** We stopped asking "how can we make this better?" and started asking "who would pay for this, and why?"

That question led us to three market opportunities:
1. Automotive (thin speakers for vehicle interiors)
2. Consumer electronics (directional audio for displays)
3. Defense (communication systems with specific acoustic properties)

Each market had different requirements. Automotive needed cost optimization and manufacturing scale. Consumer electronics needed design flexibility. Defense needed performance in specific scenarios.

We couldn't serve all three with the same product. We had to choose.

## Partnerships: Hyundai, VOXX, and the U.S. Army

### Automotive: Learning to Speak OEM

Hyundai's R&D team found us through a conference demo. They were interested in our thin-profile speakers for future vehicle designs.

Working with automotive OEMs taught me about the gap between "interested" and "purchased":

**Interested:** "This technology could work in our 2025 vehicle platform."  
**Purchased:** "We're committing to 100,000 units with these exact specifications, at this price point, with this quality standard, delivered on this schedule."

The gap between those two states was 18 months of:
- Engineering their specific acoustic requirements
- Qualifying our manufacturing process
- Negotiating pricing at scale
- Integrating with their supply chain systems

**Lesson:** Enterprise sales cycles in hardware are measured in years. Budget for them.

### Defense: Solving Real Problems

Our partnership with U.S. Army Special Operations came from a specific need: communication systems that work in RF-jammed environments.

Traditional radio communication fails when adversaries jam radio frequencies. Our directional audio technology enabled a different approach — acoustic communication that couldn't be jammed the same way.

Working with defense taught me about:

**Problem-first thinking:** They didn't care about our technology. They cared about whether their operators could communicate when everything else failed. Our job was to understand the operational context deeply enough to know which capabilities mattered.

**Roadmap selling:** They were interested not just in what we could do today, but what our architecture would enable in 2-3 years. We had to articulate a credible future without overpromising.

**Long procurement cycles:** Defense moves even slower than automotive. We needed bridge revenue while waiting for contracts.

### VOXX: Commercialization Partner

VOXX (consumer electronics distributor) became our path to market for certain applications. They had retail relationships and manufacturing scale we lacked.

This partnership taught me about **knowing what you're not good at.** We were researchers who could build prototypes. We were not operators who could manage global supply chains. Partnering let us focus on what we did well.

## Manufacturing: The Real Hard Part

Getting our technology into mass production required completely rethinking it.

A prototype that works in the lab has different constraints than a product that needs to be manufactured 100,000 times:

**Tolerance stacking:** In the lab, we could hand-tune each unit. In manufacturing, every component has variance. Those variances compound. We had to redesign for tolerance.

**Material availability:** Our prototype used a specialty adhesive from a single supplier. For manufacturing, we needed multiple qualified sources. Redesign.

**Assembly sequence:** Our lab assembly process took 2 hours per unit with skilled technicians. Manufacturing needed to be under 5 minutes with semi-skilled workers. Redesign.

**The process:** We did 6-12 month iteration loops between research prototypes in Chicago and manufacturing pilots in China. Each round taught us something that required going back to the drawing board.

**Total time from "working prototype" to "mass production":** 3 years.

## The Exit

By 2022, we had:
- 5 patents granted
- Production partnerships with major OEMs
- Manufacturing capability in China
- A team of 25

LCI Industries (NYSE: LCII) acquired us. They saw our technology as a platform for their vehicle audio systems division.

The acquisition process itself taught me about:
- Due diligence on IP (they reviewed every patent claim)
- Technology integration planning (how does this fit their roadmap)
- Team retention structures (how do you keep the inventors around)

## What I'd Do Differently

**Validate market earlier.** We spent too long on technology optimization before deeply understanding customer requirements. Earlier customer development would have focused our R&D.

**Hire manufacturing expertise earlier.** We were researchers learning manufacturing on the fly. A manufacturing-experienced hire in year 2 instead of year 4 would have accelerated our timeline significantly.

**Build bridge revenue sooner.** We relied too heavily on fundraising between enterprise sales cycles. Smaller, faster-moving customers (even at worse unit economics) would have provided cash flow stability.

## The Deep-Tech Playbook

If you're building a deep-tech company:

1. **Novel technology is the beginning, not the end.** The hard work is translating research into a product someone will buy.

2. **Pick one market to start.** You can't optimize for automotive, consumer, and defense simultaneously. Choose one, prove it, expand.

3. **Expect enterprise sales cycles measured in years.** Budget your runway accordingly.

4. **Manufacturing is a completely different discipline.** Respect it. Hire for it. Budget time for it.

5. **Partnerships can substitute for capabilities you lack.** You don't have to build everything yourself.

Deep-tech is hard. The timeline from research to revenue is long. But when it works, you've built something defensible — real intellectual property that can't be easily replicated.
